Introduction

Across the United States, many communities face long-term health consequences from industrial activity and environmental neglect. Incidents such as toxic chemical releases, lead contamination, and poor air quality highlight how pollution disproportionately affects low-income areas and people of color.

Understanding these disparities is critical for identifying where policy and intervention are most needed. By examining the relationship between pollution exposure, socioeconomic factors, and health outcomes, this project seeks to uncover patterns of environmental injustice and health disparities.

Studying these connections not only reveals the human cost of industrial and governmental oversight but also emphasizes the urgent need for equitable public health protections and sustainable environmental practices.

Data Sources and Methodology

This analysis combines multiple county-level datasets to examine environmental health disparities across the United States. Our data sources include comprehensive environmental, demographic, and health indicators necessary to identify patterns of environmental injustice.

Socioeconomic Data

  • Poverty Data 2023 (~3,000 counties)
  • Corrected county demographics
  • 2024 US census data

Environmental Exposure

  • HDPulse environmental data export
  • Corrected facilities data
  • Integrated environmental indicators

Health Outcomes

  • County COPD prevalence data
  • Incidence and mortality rates (incd.csv)
  • Merged final health indicators

Data Processing Methodology

All datasets were merged using Federal Information Processing Standards (FIPS) codes to ensure accurate county-level alignment. Missing data was handled through careful imputation and outlier analysis. Variables were standardized for cross-county comparison and converted to geospatial format for mapping applications.

Air Pollution vs. COPD Prevalence

A positive correlation exists between air pollution and COPD rates, with areas having higher industrial facility counts (shown in yellow/green) showing elevated pollution levels and respiratory health impacts.

Key Findings and Implications

Environmental Justice Patterns

Our analysis reveals clear patterns of environmental injustice, with low-income communities and communities of color experiencing disproportionately high exposure to environmental hazards including air pollution, toxic facilities, and water quality violations.

Health Outcome Disparities

Counties with higher pollution exposure show significantly elevated rates of cancer and COPD, with the strongest correlations observed in areas with lower median household incomes, highlighting the compounding effects of environmental and socioeconomic disadvantage.

Geographic Clustering

Environmental health disparities cluster geographically, particularly in industrial regions of the Southeast and areas with legacy pollution sources, suggesting the need for targeted regional policy interventions.

Policy Recommendations

  1. Targeted Environmental Justice Enforcement: Prioritize enforcement of environmental regulations in counties identified as having high pollution exposure and vulnerable populations.
  2. Health Monitoring Programs: Implement enhanced health monitoring and early intervention programs in high-risk counties to address the elevated rates of cancer and COPD.
  3. Community Investment: Direct infrastructure and economic development investments toward disadvantaged communities to address the underlying socioeconomic factors that exacerbate environmental health disparities.

Future Research Directions

Future analysis could incorporate longitudinal health data to track changes over time, examine the effectiveness of specific policy interventions, and investigate additional environmental factors such as proximity to hazardous waste sites and climate change impacts on vulnerable communities.
